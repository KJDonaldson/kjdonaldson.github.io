## Overview

My research focuses on how organisms respond to changing environments, and how experience-dependent plasticity shapes the range of possible adaptive responses. I am interested in plasticity as a multiscale process that operates over different timescales, from rapid changes in behavior and neural processing to forms of structural and cellular reorganization that emerge when environmental pressures persist.

Across several systems, I have examined how environmental context influences behavior and sensory processing, particularly how learning and sustained experience alter the way organisms perceive stimuli and use that information to guide future actions. These changes can be expressed behaviorally, through learned strategies and internal representations, and physiologically, through experience-dependent modulation of sensory and neural responses. Rather than viewing these effects in isolation, I approach them as coordinated adjustments that allow organisms to flexibly interpret and act on their environments.

In parallel, I have studied how sustained challenges such as aging, environmental stress, or disease states drive plastic change at the tissue and structural level. Using quantitative imaging and computational analysis, I have examined how sensory tissues remodel in response to these pressures, including in genetic models that alter baseline cellular function. This work has also motivated my interest in the molecular bases of plasticity, and in integrating structural and physiological measurements with gene- and pathway-level analyses in future research.

## Physiological and Behavioral Plasticity in Sensory Systems

A central component of my research has focused on how sensory systems adapt to changing environmental demands through experience-dependent changes in physiology and behavior. I am interested in how sensory processing is shaped by both learning and sustained environmental exposure, such that neural responses come to reflect the relevance of stimuli for guiding behavior rather than simply their physical properties.

In mammalian systems, I examined how sensory and association cortices contribute to flexible sensory processing. In work involving orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and auditory cortex, I contributed to physiological recordings demonstrating that OFC neurons respond robustly to sound even in the absence of explicit training. Related experiments showed that OFC can directly influence auditory cortical responses, supporting a model in which higher-order cortical areas modulate early sensory processing as environmental context or behavioral relevance changes.

While studying these forms of cortical plasticity, I became increasingly interested in the underlying changes that allow sensory processing to remain flexible over time. Observing experience-dependent shifts in neural responses raised broader questions about how such changes are supported and maintained within biological systems. These questions motivated my transition to genetically tractable models in which experience, neural activity, and cellular change could be examined in closer alignment.

In Drosophila, I pursued these questions in the context of thermosensation, focusing on cold and cold-nociceptive pathways. Using physiological recordings, quantitative behavioral analysis, and structural morphology, I showed that repeated sensory experience alters behavioral dynamics as well as the functional properties of primary cold-sensing neurons. Changes in sensory neuron physiology were observed using both calcium imaging and electrophysiology in animals reared under different thermal conditions, demonstrating that environmental history directly shapes sensory processing. Structural analyses provided additional evidence of experience-dependent remodeling in this system.

Together, these studies reflect my broader interest in how experience reshapes sensory systems across levels of organization, from neural activity and behavior to cellular and structural change. This body of work has guided my ongoing interest in identifying the molecular programs that support and stabilize plasticity, and in pursuing approaches that link physiological and structural adaptation to underlying genetic and regulatory mechanisms.
